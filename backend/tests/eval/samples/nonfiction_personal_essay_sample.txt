On Leaving the Algorithm

Section 1: The Morning I Deleted Everything

I remember the exact moment I decided to leave. It was a Tuesday morning in November, the kind of gray Pacific Northwest day where the clouds sit so low they feel like a ceiling. I was lying in bed, and before I had even opened my eyes fully, my hand had found my phone. My thumb was already scrolling before I was conscious enough to form a thought.

That in itself was not unusual. I had been starting every morning that way for at least five years. What was different that Tuesday was that I noticed it — really noticed it, the way you sometimes hear a clock ticking in a room and realize it has been ticking the entire time. My first waking act, every single day, was to check what strangers thought about things that did not concern me. I was reading opinions before I had formed my own opinion about whether I wanted to be awake.

I sat up and deleted Instagram, Twitter, and TikTok. Then I deleted Reddit, which I told myself was different because it was "information" rather than "social media," but which I knew, in the way you know things you have been lying to yourself about, was the worst of all of them. The whole process took about forty seconds. I had expected to feel something dramatic — liberation, anxiety, the digital equivalent of jumping out of a plane. Instead I felt slightly bored, which in retrospect was the most honest possible reaction. I had just removed the thing that had been preventing me from being bored for half a decade.

Section 2: What Boredom Taught Me

The first week was genuinely difficult, but not in the way I expected. I did not miss the content. I did not care about the discourse, the discourse about the discourse, or any of the nested layers of reaction that constitute the modern internet experience. What I missed was the absence of silence. My brain had been trained to reach for stimulation the moment any gap appeared — waiting for coffee, standing in line, the thirty seconds between deciding to do something and actually starting to do it. Those gaps had been filled for so long that encountering them empty felt like stepping off a curb that was higher than I expected.

By the second week, something shifted. The boredom stopped feeling like deprivation and started feeling like space. I began to notice things in my neighborhood that I must have walked past thousands of times: the wisteria that had completely consumed someone's mailbox, the way the light hit the brick building on 12th Avenue at four in the afternoon. These are embarrassingly small observations. I am aware of that. But their smallness is precisely the point. The algorithm had recalibrated my attention toward the large, the urgent, the outrage-worthy, and the heartwarming-in-exactly-the-right-proportion. It had no use for wisteria on a mailbox.

My reading changed too. I had been a compulsive consumer of articles and threads — thousands of words a day, probably tens of thousands — but I realized I could not remember what I had read even a week later. The information moved through me like water through a sieve. When I started reading books again, physical books with covers and pages, the experience was almost uncomfortably immersive. My attention span had atrophied so dramatically that reading for thirty minutes felt like an endurance sport. But it was coming back. By the end of the first month, I was finishing a book every week or two, and I could remember what was in them.

Section 3: The Social Costs and Unexpected Gains

Leaving social media is, in American culture in the year 2025, a mildly antisocial act. People reacted to it the way they might react if I told them I had stopped using electricity — a mixture of admiration, suspicion, and the faint sense that I was making a statement about them by implication. Several friends told me they wished they could do the same thing, which frustrated me because they could. The phone was right there in their hands. The delete button worked for everyone.

But I understand the hesitation, because the social costs are real. I missed a friend's pregnancy announcement. I did not know about a former colleague's new job. I was not invited to a party that was organized entirely through Instagram DMs. These are the small threads that, collectively, constitute the fabric of contemporary social connection, and pulling yourself out of the weave means accepting that some of those threads will fray.

What I gained in return was harder to quantify but, I believe, more substantial. My relationships with the people I saw in person became richer, not because I was suddenly a better conversationalist but because I had things to talk about that were not simply reactions to things we had both already seen online. Conversations regained the quality of discovery. When a friend told me about something that had happened in the news, it was actually news to me, and my response was my own rather than a rehearsed version of whatever take I had already absorbed from my feed.

Section 4: On Attention as a Moral Question

I want to be careful here, because the "I quit social media and it changed my life" essay is its own genre now, complete with its own conventions and predictable beats. I am aware that I am, at this moment, writing one of those essays. But I think the conversation about technology and attention has been too narrowly framed as a question of personal productivity or mental health. Those things matter, but they are symptoms of a deeper issue.

The deeper issue is that attention is the medium through which we construct our experience of being alive. What we attend to is, in a very real sense, who we are. When we outsource the direction of our attention to an algorithm whose objective function is engagement — which in practice means emotional activation, which in practice means anxiety, outrage, and desire — we are not just wasting time. We are allowing a profit-maximizing system to determine the texture of our inner lives.

This is not a conspiracy theory. No one at these companies is twirling a mustache. The people who build these systems are, by and large, thoughtful individuals who use their own products and feel the same ambivalence about them that everyone else does. The problem is structural, not moral. The incentive structure of attention-based business models is fundamentally misaligned with human flourishing, and no amount of screen-time reminders or grayscale mode is going to fix that misalignment.

Section 5: One Year Later

It has been a little over a year now since that Tuesday morning. I will not pretend that my life has been transformed in some Hollywood montage sort of way. I still waste time. I still procrastinate. I still occasionally find myself reading Wikipedia articles about obscure historical events at two in the morning when I should be sleeping. The human capacity for distraction does not require a smartphone; it just requires a human.

But something has changed in the quality of my attention, and by extension in the quality of my daily experience. I am more present — not in the mindfulness-app, branded-meditation sense of the word, but in the simple sense that I am more often aware of where I am and what I am doing while I am doing it. The constant background hum of other people's curated lives has gone quiet, and in the silence I have found something that I can only describe as my own frequency.

I do not think everyone should delete their social media accounts. I think everyone should ask themselves whether the way they spend their attention reflects the kind of person they want to be. For me, the answer was no, and the solution was drastic. For someone else, it might be different. But the question itself — what am I paying attention to, and why? — strikes me as one of the most important questions a person can ask in an era designed, at every level, to prevent them from asking it.
